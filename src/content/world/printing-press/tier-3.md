For most of history, reproducing a text meant copying it by hand — the painstaking work of scribes,
often monks in monastery scriptoria. A single Bible could take a scribe more than a year. Books were
therefore scarce, costly, and largely confined to the Church and the wealthy.

Around 1450 in Mainz, **Johannes Gutenberg** combined several innovations into a practical system:
reusable **movable type** cast from a metal alloy, an oil-based ink that clung to metal, and a press
adapted from those used to press grapes and olives. The result let a workshop print hundreds of
identical copies of a page in the time a scribe needed for one.

The consequences were enormous. Printed books spread literacy, standardized spelling and texts, and
let new ideas circulate across Europe within weeks rather than generations. Historians often mark
the printing press as one of the hinges between the medieval and the modern world.
