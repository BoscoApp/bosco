## The red fox (_Vulpes vulpes_)

The red fox is the most widespread wild member of the dog family (Canidae) in the world, found
across North America, Europe, Asia, and even introduced to Australia. Despite belonging to the
dogs, it has several cat-like traits: vertical-slit pupils that help it hunt at dawn and dusk, and
partially retractable claws that give it a light, pouncing step.

### Senses and hunting

A fox's hearing is extraordinary. It can detect the low-frequency rustling of a small animal
moving through grass or beneath snow, and it locates prey by sound with remarkable precision. When
it strikes, it performs the high, arcing leap naturalists call "mousing," pinning prey with its
forepaws. Foxes are _omnivores_ and _opportunists_ — their diet shifts with the season, from
rodents and rabbits in winter to insects, earthworms, and fruit in summer. This flexibility is the
key to their success in so many habitats.

### Life cycle and family

Red foxes are largely solitary outside the breeding season. In late winter a mated pair establishes
a territory; the vixen bears a litter of four to six kits in spring, in a den often borrowed and
enlarged from another animal's burrow. Both parents provision the young, who emerge to play near
the den entrance before dispersing in autumn to establish territories of their own.
